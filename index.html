One - Novel Excerpt

At last one lifts one’s head, and another dream disappears—where does it go? One reaches back for it in one’s mind—and reactivates a thought, something else entirely, a glittering image. Something quivers inside it, an inertia, a gravity. What here is tickled? What motions have commenced? Something gathers, rises, cusps over the edge, surges …

 

A family vacation. One is young—ten, eleven, perhaps. This trip, one’s parents insist, a milky fondness in their eyes, is a gift, a freedom, a time to be enjoyed. To one, above all it is a time to be with one’s eldest sibling, the idol of one’s youth, who one looks up to with an unreasoned, unconsidered fervor, who one worships as a way themselves. What one’s sibling desires enters the world as one’s desires fluidly, as if there were no media nor mind traversed in the transference, as if it belonged to both at the moment of its conception … the cottage one’s family rents is tucked into a sleepy hamlet near a beach whose refined white sand has blown into the turns and gutters and cracks of the streets—the whiteness pales the hanging, sinewy verdure, abundant but salted-thin, brittle and sand-choked. Copses and leaf-strewn paths branch off every chalky road—the decaying tunnels wend through properties whose demarcations the sands and the winds have long ago effaced … one’s family had taken an isolated way that pathed through overgrown groves, gray-green, thick with thistle and lichen and loose-barked sea-birch, and opened onto a pristine, secluded beach. Here, the ocean, graded from the earthy hues, emerged a quiet infinity, eliciting, even then—for one was young—grand conceptions of oneself. Here—and even then—one howled with the fierce wind of identity; one hummed the quiet lullabies of thought—and so did one's sibling, first, irrevocably … one’s sibling wishes to take one, one alone, to this beach—and so it is one’s desire as well. One’s parents worry, avoid the question. But one’s sibling insists. At last, despite themselves, one parent concludes it’s safe enough: the kids, they’re older now, it’s time they, their parents, realized that. The other parent looks on worryingly, knowing something and feeling another, saying nothing … and so it is allowed. With great fanfare one and one’s sibling begin. As the property’s threshold is crossed, something changes, something inside changes. Adults now, humans now, one and one’s sibling begin to march—one’s sibling knows the way to the path; of course, mature, one's sibling has memorized it, has observed the world, internalized its knowledge—one knows this. One follows willingly, gladly: alone, on a foreign road, walking with one’s idol, conquering the world, becoming alive—could one ask for more? One doesn’t think of what sunlight remains in the day, though it is late: an hour of walking flies by in sheer delight. There is nothing to fear, for one is following one’s messiah to a promised land, a new existence … another hour passes. Night begins to descend. One’s sibling, to one’s shock, begins to cry. The hamlet is small, there are no road signs, the streets are all similar: another brown-paneled house dilapidating under the steady pressure of sea weather, another white shack greening at the gills—this at every corner. Paths to the beach suggest themselves—one copse beckons, another; one’s sibling suggests one is near, though one’s sibling is weeping. And then the confession—I do not know where we are anymore … the myth fractures. Suddenly one knows fear. One’s mind leaps into the unknown: one is sure to die, one knows what it means to be alone and die; one loses one’s name in the suffocating fear. There is no hope—for one’s sibling cries too, a hopeless wail. An hour of tears … not a soul passes. The sand whips up and around, settles in the nostrils, hair, teeth. Night now. One and one’s sibling must move. One chooses left because one has not chosen anything for hours—but the path is long foregone. One only wants home; desperately, one wants one’s parents. The night thickens and despair settles in, true despair, as red and blue lights fire around a corner. The shriek booms after their flares …

 

One forgets, having been destroyed, what happens next, save this: next to one’s idol, in the backseat of a car, one cries not for the missed path, but for one’s sibling’s fallenness.

 

One gets up. One dresses in one’s drab, half-tattered habit, one eats one’s bleak breakfast. One packs a spartan lunch, one trudges unwashed out of one’s apartment. One’s neighborhood is spartan and unwashed, the train one takes southward to the Office is spartan and unwashed. The train descends, rises up from its descent—the second and greater of one’s city’s two grand effluvia emerges glistening and churning in the morning light. The train descends once again. One finds a seat, stands back up. One conceives of a Work—a fantasia of subjectivity—one destroys the Work. The doors open, one climbs the filthy station steps up into the entropic crush of street life. One makes the short, disorienting walk to the Office.
